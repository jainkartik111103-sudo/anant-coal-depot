Quintal, Tonne, Truck Load: Coal Units and Weights Explained

The Indian coal trade quotes in units that confuse first-time buyers: quintals, tonnes, tempo loads, truck loads. Two minutes here and you will speak the trade's language — and order exactly what you mean to.

Quick answer

In the Indian coal trade: 1 quintal = 100 kg, and 1 tonne = 1,000 kg = 10 quintals. Small retail purchases are quoted in kg or bags, mid-size orders in quintals, bulk orders in tonnes, and industrial supply in tempo or truck loads — where the load depends on the vehicle's carrying capacity, roughly a few tonnes for a tempo and considerably more for full-size trucks. When ordering, state the product, the quantity in one of these units, and your delivery location; the depot converts and quotes from there.

Note on bags: unlike quintals and tonnes, a "bag" is not a standard weight — it varies by product and packer. If you are comparing quotes, compare per kg, per quintal or per tonne, never per bag.

Quick worked examples

  • "5 quintal hard coke" = 500 kg.
  • "2 tonne kacha koyla" = 2,000 kg = 20 quintals.
  • "Half tonne charcoal" = 500 kg = 5 quintals.

Which unit should you order in?

Order in the unit that matches your storage and consumption, not the biggest one you can afford. A dhaba that burns a quintal of charcoal a week is better served by a recurring 1–2 quintal delivery than a giant one-time pile stored badly. A brick kiln consuming hard coke by the tonne should order truck loads and negotiate as a bulk buyer. If you tell us your consumption pattern, we will suggest the ordering rhythm — that is normal practice, not a special request.

How vehicle loads are decided

"Tempo load" and "truck load" are shorthand for the carrying capacity of the vehicle, so the exact tonnage depends on which vehicle runs your delivery.
