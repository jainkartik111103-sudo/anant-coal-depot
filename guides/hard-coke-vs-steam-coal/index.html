Hard Coke vs Steam Coal: Which Should You Buy?

Two of the most asked-for fuels at our depot are hard coke and steam coal (kacha koyla). They look similar in a heap, but they behave very differently once lit. This guide explains the difference in plain language so you order the right fuel the first time.

Quick answer

Hard coke is a processed, high-carbon fuel that burns hot, steady and with little smoke — the standard choice for furnaces, bhattis and brick kilns. Steam coal is raw mined coal (kacha koyla) that ignites more easily and costs less per unit, but burns with more smoke and less uniform heat — typically used for boilers, steam generation and general industrial heating. If your work involves melting, forging or firing at sustained high heat, buy hard coke; if you are raising steam or need general heat, steam coal usually makes more sense.

What each fuel actually is

Hard coke is not raw coal. It is made by heating coking coal in ovens until the smoky, volatile part is driven off, leaving dense, high-carbon lumps. That processing is what gives coke its character: it lights slower than raw coal, but once the bed is glowing it holds an intense, even heat for a long time with very little smoke or flame. In the local trade it is also called pathar koyla, chota koyla or bada koyla depending on lump size.

Steam coal is raw coal as it comes from the mine, screened but not processed. Locally it is called kacha koyla — "raw coal". Because its volatile matter is still in the lump, it catches fire more readily and burns with visible flame and noticeably more smoke. It is the workhorse fuel for boilers and general heating, and it is usually the more economical of the two per unit weight.

How to decide for your use

  • Melting, forging or firing at sustained high heat — furnaces, bhattis, brick kilns, metal work: choose hard coke. The steady, smokeless bed is exactly what these processes need. Then pick the lump size: chota for tighter fuel beds and smaller openings, bada for larger bhattis.
  • Raising steam or general heating — boilers, dryers, heating applications: steam coal is usually the economical, practical choice.
  • Cost-sensitive kiln or boiler firing — ask about hard coke dust (coke breeze), the fine grade of coke that many brick kilns and boiler operators blend in to bring fuel cost down.
  • Cooking, tandoors and grills — neither. For food you want natural wood charcoal (lakdi koyla), which is a different product altogether.
